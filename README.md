Repository for data analyses to examine effects of trampling disturbance associated with trails on alpine plant species richness, diversity 
and species composition in the south Coast mountains, Garibaldi Provincial Park. 

Aims: This study examines the effects of trampling and other associated recreational
impacts, on biodiversity of coast mountain heather meadows, around T'ak't'ak'múy'in tl'a
In'inyáxa7n (Black Tusk, in Garibaldi provincial park, around British Columbia, Canada. We
studied the growth and reproductive traits of 4 taxa within the system in 2022 surveys (Chardon
et al. 2023); In 2023 we conducted preliminary species compositional surveys of the plots to
understand trampling effects on the wider vascular plant communities.
In 2024, were refined our survey technique and taxonomic documentation (as below) for the
present study.

In summer 2024, we re-surveyed our long-term vegetation sampling transects (Chardon et al.
2023) for species richness, augmenting our procedures from the previous field season as follows:
a) We adopted percent cover metrics (rather than pres/abs used in 2023).
b) We identified graminoids and bryophytes to species level, with the assistance of local
bryologist and resources at the Beaty museum (dissecting microscope and floras).
In 2025, we also completed our proposed invasive species survey along trails, and resurveyed
plots and collected samples where an invasive grass had been documented the previous season to
confirm this finding.

We thus continue our goal to quantify the continued effects of human trampling on these plant
and microbial communities by returning each year, thus generating a multi-year dataset. Such a
dataset will allow us to answer how these plant communities are responding to continuous
trampling and ambient warming.

Procedures:
Field sampling setup: Relocated permanent transects along Taylor Meadows Trail, Black Tusk
Trail, and Panorama Ridge Trail using GPS points on Gaia app as reference; Reference GPS
points, 2022 transect photos, and 2022 photo bearing to find exact locations of transects. Placed
new, labeled stake at starting point, and placed 10m transect tape out from 0 m to 10 m, using
flags to hold transect in place. Took new transect photo from starting point, labelled clear
laminated sheets with date, location (TM, BT, or PR), transect number, and bearing in the
direction the photo is taken. Made sure that distinguishable landscape features are seen in
picture. Compared new photos with photos from previous to ensure identical alignment, and use
compass to shoot the same bearing as in original photo.

Vegetation Sampling: Set out 50 cm x 100 cm quadrat at every meter. Quadrats were placed
over plot, and used to define the area for percent cover surveys. Recorded unique transect
number and quadrat number; Additional details about the transects themselves (slope
information, aspect, community codes).

2024 surveys: We recorded the presence and percent cover of all vascular plant species and total moss cover within 10 contiguous, 1.0 m by 0.5 m subplots for
the 14 transect pairs. Bryophytes (mosses, liverworts) were sampled to species level for a subset
of six transect pairs, with identifications confirmed morphologically in both the field and
laboratory by a local bryologist, and total (undifferentiated by species) bryophyte cover
was recorded in field for all other transects for which our moss expert did not accompany field
sampling. To indicate ground disturbance, cover of bare mineral soil (denuded substrate), litter-
covered soil, rock (with or without lichen cover), and cryptogamic crust-covered soil was also
recorded. Cover classes were <1% (later keyed as 0.5), 1%, 5%, then five-percent intervals
between 5% and 50% and ten-percent intervals between 50% and 100%. To improve accuracy
and consistency, observers worked in pairs and referenced standardized cover diagrams. Surveys
were conducted in mid-July to early August when most meadow species are in bloom and
identifiable. Species identification was based on prior knowledge of morphology and
supplemented by regional floras (Pojar and MacKinnon 2021). Voucher specimens were
collected for taxa with subtler identifying features requiring confirmation under a dissecting
microscope, notably graminoids and bryophytes. Lab identification was guided by detailed
floristic keys (Douglas et al. 2001), additional resources, and personnel at the Beaty Biodiversity
Research Centre. Follow-up surveys targeting invasive species were made to confirm plot
observations and broaden the area documented.

2025 surveys: We revisited plots in Taylor to confirm sightings of invasive species recorded in
2024; We also searched the entire transect (trailside-trampled and reference-undisturbed) for any
further instances and recorded these whether in or immediately adjacent to plots. We further
conducted a survey along trails in the study system searching for instances of any invasive
species and recorded these in the Gaia GPS app. We took photos and voucher samples of the
invasive species and examined these in the lab under dissecting scope at a later date.

## Compaction Measures
Trampling Study, Garibaldi Provincial Park (Summer 2023)
#-----------------------------

Aims: In summer 2023, we returned to re-survey each transect for species richness and to measure soil compaction with a soil penetrometer. We aim to quantify the continued effects of
human trampling on these plant and microbial communities by returning each year, thus generating a multi-year dataset. Such a dataset will allow us to answer how these plant
communities are responding to continuous trampling and ambient warming. We will incorporate BC Parks data on visitation numbers in our analyses to answer how strongly yearly
visitation rates correlate with community responses, or if these responses show more of a lag response to past visitation rates.

#-----------------------------
## Data collection notes summer 2023 

Procedures:
Field sampling setup: Relocated permanent transects along Taylor Meadows Trail, Black Tusk Trail, and Panorama Ridge Trail using GPS points on Gaia app as reference; Reference GPS
points, 2022 transect photos, and 2022 photo bearing to find exact locations of transects. Placed new, labeled stake at starting point, and placed 10m transect tape out from 0 m to 10 m,
using flags to hold transect in place. Took new transect photo from starting point, labelled clear laminated sheets with date, location (TM, BT, or PR), transect number, and bearing in the
direction the photo is taken. Made sure that distinguishable landscape features are seen in picture. Compared new photos with photos from previous to ensure identical alignment, and
use compass to shoot the same bearing as in original photo.

Quantifying soil compaction: At every transect, measured compaction with a soil pocket penetrometer (Gilson HM-500) to quantify soil compaction non-destructively as surface
penetration resistance (kg  cm⁻²), or compressive strength, with accuracy to 0.122 kg cm⁻² (Graesch et al. 2015). Higher values indicate greater compaction. One measurement was taken
at the centre of each subplot. If obstructed (e.g., by a large root or rock), sampling was shifted 10 cm to the left until suitable substrate was found, and if obstruction persisted to the quadrat
edge, steps were repeated on the right side, targeting mineral soil (bare, litter- or crust-covered). Plots dominated by exceptionally stoney substrates were omitted as they did not
represent mineral soil, a situation that occurred at only one high-elevation transect pair on the Black tusk trail. Since the penetrometer measures strength of confined soils rather than loose
debris, loose woody material or recently displaced dust trail dust was gently brushed aside with a soft paintbrush to expose mineral soil prior to measurement. Measurements were taken
through cryptogamic crust, including moss-containing areas, as these represent a rooting surface, and their presence noted. Where resistance occasionally exceeded 4.5 kg cm⁻² (the
instrument maximum), readings were conservatively set to recorded as &gt;4.75 (units) for analyses. 

Vegetation Sampling: Set out 50 cm x 100 cm quadrat at every meter. Quadrats were placed over plot, with strings such that sub-quadrats were every 25 cm for 4x25 cm x 50 cm
subquadrats per quadrat. Recorded unique transect number and quadrat number; Identified plant species and indicated presence (1) or absence (0) in each sub-quadrat. Took photos and
voucher samples of species hard to identify and indicate what transect-quad and any distinguishing features on the laminated sheet. These were examined in the lab at a later date.

References:
Graesch AP, Shankel SE, Schaepe DM. The Pocket Penetrometer: An Onsite Method for Discerning the Presence of Earthen House Floors and Other Trampled Surfaces. Advances in
Archaeological Practice. 2015;3(2):93-106. doi:10.7183/2326-3768.3.2.93
